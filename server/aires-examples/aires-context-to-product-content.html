From context to product content

Process · June 2026 · Upstream companion to "Requirements gathering & defining scope" · A living document

What this isHow we turn a raw context dump — a brain dump, a sales call, a client signal — into structured product content: a thesis, a diagram pack, and scoped tickets. This sits upstream of Requirements gathering & defining scope and hands off to it.
01

Why this matters

Our richest product input is unstructured: a founder's stream of consciousness, a thing a client said, the texture of how a rep actually works. That raw context is gold — and it usually dies. It rots in a Slack thread, or it gets "cleaned up" into something tidy that has lost the very insight that made it valuable.

Good context work does three things:

  • Preserves the texture — the offhand line ("needle in a haystack") is often the whole thesis.
  • Forces a thesis — a point of view, not just a feature list, so everything downstream has a spine.
  • Connects to delivery — context that never becomes a ticket was entertainment, not product work.
PrincipleDistill before you decorate. Find the argument first. Diagrams and docs are how you communicate a thesis — they are not a substitute for having one.
02

The pipeline

Five stages, each producing a concrete artifact and feeding the next. Run it linearly the first time; iterate freely after.

Stage 01

Capture

Get the raw context down without tidying it. Keep the verbatim phrases.

→ Context note
Stage 02

Distill

Extract the core bet. Problem before solution. One point of view.

→ Thesis doc
Stage 03

Visualize

Draw each beat of the thesis as the right diagram type.

→ Diagram pack
Stage 04

Scope

Hand to requirements gathering: MoSCoW, vertical slices, INVEST.

→ Scoped tickets
Stage 05

Ship & learn

Build the thin slice, watch the metric, feed learnings back to the thesis.

→ Working software
03

Principles

  • Problem before solution. Context usually arrives as solutions ("saved filters", "a dashboard"). Dig back to the underlying job before committing to a "how" — the same instinct as the 5 whys in requirements gathering.
  • One thesis, many artifacts. Every diagram and doc should trace to a single line in the thesis. If it doesn't, it's noise.
  • Preserve the texture. Carry the user's own words through. A vivid phrase out-communicates a paragraph of abstraction.
  • Diagrams are arguments. A diagram should make a claim, not just inventory boxes. Pick the type that makes the claim.
  • Make the implicit explicit. The dump assumes context you must write down — stakeholders, surfaces, edge cases — or it can't be scoped.
  • Stop at "ready to slice." Context work is done when requirements gathering can take over, not before and not after.
04

Choosing the right diagram for the beat

Each thesis beat has a natural diagram. Match the claim you're making to the form that carries it.

Thesis beat / claimDiagramWhy this form
"Here's the user's real job"JTBD canvasSeparates functional / emotional / social jobs from solutions
"This compounds / self-reinforces"FlywheelShows a loop, not a line
"Two stakeholders, one truth"PersonasHolds two viewpoints side by side
"This is the daily flow / decisions"User flowBranches, loops, and the "done" state
"Surfaces + the engine beneath"Service blueprintFrontstage vs backstage vs support
"Same capability, different needs"Capability matrixCapabilities × segments, priority per cell
"What to build first"Impact-effort / MoSCoWForces prioritization, not a wish-list
05

Handoffs

The chainThis doc → requirements gathering & defining scope → definition of done. Context work produces the thesis and the diagram pack; requirements gathering turns them into scoped, sliced, INVEST tickets; definition of done governs when each ticket is actually finished. Don't skip the middle step — a thesis is not a ticket.
06

Anti-patterns

  • Thesis-less diagrams — pretty boxes with no argument; decoration mistaken for thinking.
  • The diagram zoo — ten diagrams that each say a little; pick the few that each carry one beat.
  • Tidying away the gold — sanding off the vivid phrase that was the actual insight.
  • Solutioning in the thesis — locking the "how" before the "why" is even written.
  • Context that never ships — a beautiful deck that never reaches a ticket or a metric.
  • Skipping scope — jumping from thesis straight to building, with no in / out / non-goals.
07

Quick checklist

  • The raw context is captured verbatim, texture intact
  • A single thesis / core bet is written as a point of view
  • Every diagram traces to one beat of that thesis
  • The implicit is explicit: users, surfaces, stakeholders, edge cases
  • It's been handed to requirements gathering for slicing
  • A success metric is named so "ship & learn" can close the loop
08

Worked example — this very exercise

MetaThis process produced what you're reading.
  • Capture: a founder brain dump — "highest impact in least time", "data in / data out", "needle in a haystack", PATH / Peterson / Creo, three surfaces.
  • Distill: → the rep velocity thesis (results lag, so velocity is the currency; the day must be self-directing).
  • Visualize: → JTBD canvas, data flywheel, personas, daily flow, service blueprint, client capability matrix — one per beat.
  • Scope: → rep velocity — scoped requirements: MoSCoW + 11 vertically-sliced INVEST tickets with acceptance criteria.
  • Ship & learn: → pull the thin slice (saved filters → Smart List → reportable activity), watch time-to-first-action and velocity, feed back.

When new context extends the thesis

A second brain dump arrived — the pull job: intent-driven filtering and outreach ("everyone who opened the two-bedroom promo," "Jason's A & B prospects while he's away"). The right move wasn't to start over; it was to extend. We distilled the new beat (push vs. pull), drew the artifacts that carry it (an intent JTBD canvas and an intent → list → outreach capability map), scoped it (intent-driven lists & outreach requirements), and then threaded that scope back through the original set so the thesis, personas, flow, blueprint, and matrix all reflect both modes. Same pipeline, run as an amendment rather than a rewrite.

GroundingBuilt on the same practice base as our requirements work: problem-before-solution and the 5 whys; specification by example; jobs-to-be-done; story mapping; and the principle that clarity is cheapest at the front of the funnel.
